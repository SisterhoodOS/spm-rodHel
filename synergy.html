How to Frame It Before You Speak
Internal posture first · The frame that lets this land cleanly
You are not demoting her. You are not rejecting her. You are giving her a real role with real scope instead of an ambiguous one that has been generating confusion for both of you. The conversation fails when Sophia enters it already apologizing. It lands when Sophia enters it from the certainty that clarity is the most respectful thing she can offer.
Lead with recognition
Start with what is genuinely true about what she has contributed. This is not flattery — it is the accurate case. She got you to a first sale. The masterclass framework was real strategic thinking. Name it before anything else. This is not strategy, it is just honest.
Name the role, not the demotion
The reframe is not "you are less than I thought." The reframe is "there is a specific role that uses your actual strengths, and I have been letting the framing drift past what that role is." Name what the role IS, not just what it is not.
Then be quiet
After the main statement, stop talking. The instinct is to keep explaining until she agrees. That instinct creates negotiation space. Say it. Then sit in the silence. She needs to process, not be persuaded.

The Full Script
What to say · In Sophia's voice · No em dashes · No hedging · No apologizing
Opening — Lead with what is real
"Helena, I want to be honest with you because I respect what you have brought to this. You got me to my first sale. The masterclass framework was real strategic thinking. You can hold a room, run a sales conversation, and show up on camera without flinching. These are not small things and I have not been clear enough that I see them."
Let this land before continuing. Do not rush to the next part.
The Shift — Say it simply, without dressing it up
"What I have not been clear about is what role this actually is for you. I let the framing drift into co-creation when the reality is different. And I think that gap has made things harder for both of us, not easier."
This is accountability without apology. You are naming what happened without collapsing under it.
The Role — Name it directly
"What I need, and what I believe actually fits your strengths, is a defined role with scope. You lead the masterclass facilitation. You run the sales conversations. You execute the launch campaigns. You get compensated well for that work. That is real, substantive work and it is the work where you are genuinely excellent."
The Boundary — State it once, clearly, without softening
"What it is not: co-founder. Not equity. Not co-creator of the vision. I hold that seat. I am telling you this clearly because the ambiguity has been harder on you than clarity would be. You deserve to know exactly what you are being asked to build and what you are not."
Then stop. Let her respond. Do not pre-empt the silence with more words.
How to Hold What Comes Back
"But I thought we were building this together."
"I know. I let that framing live longer than it should have. That is on me. This is me getting clear now." Then stop. Do not keep explaining.
"So what do I actually get from this?"
"Compensation for the sales and facilitation role. A defined scope where your strengths are actually used. Work that converts your contribution into real results. If you want a piece of something long-term, build in this role for a year and we revisit from there. But that conversation starts when the role is working, not before we have figured out what the role is."
She goes quiet or visibly upset.
"I know this is not what you were expecting to hear. Take the time you need to sit with it." Then let the silence be. Silence after a hard truth is not failure. It is the truth landing.
"I need to think about whether this is still what I want."
"That is a fair response. Take the time. I would like to know where you land by [specific date]. What I cannot do is continue in the space we have been in." Name a date. The indefinite "take your time" creates more limbo.
She says yes immediately.
"Good. Let's define the scope and compensation today. I want this to be clear and fair." Move directly into the practical conversation while the clarity is alive. Do not celebrate and then leave it vague again.
What Sophia Must Not Say
Do Not Say
"Eventually you could have equity." This creates a moving goalpost and tells her the current structure is temporary. She will operate inside it waiting for it to change.
Do Not Say
"I see you as a co-founder, just not right now." Same problem as above. The "not right now" becomes the entire focus. She will not hear anything else.
Do Not Say
"I need to think about what this means for us." Sophia does the thinking before the conversation. Walking in already decided is what makes the conversation clean. If Sophia is not decided, do not have the conversation yet.
Do Not Say
"I value you so much, you are so important to me." Without also naming what changes, this sounds like the setup to something that never lands. The affirmation has to come with the clarity, not instead of it.
Do Not Say
"This is just business, nothing personal." It is personal and she knows it. Pretending otherwise will feel dismissive and cause more resistance. Keep the warmth. Pair it with the boundary.
After the Conversation
Send her a short written summary of what was agreed within 24 hours. Not a legal document, not a formal contract yet. Just: "Following our conversation, here is how I understand what we agreed: [role, scope, compensation structure]. Let me know if you see it differently." This creates a reference point and closes the loop before anything can be reinterpreted through the emotional lens of Mercury/Moon. The written record is not a lack of trust. It is the respect of making the clarity permanent rather than leaving it to memory.
